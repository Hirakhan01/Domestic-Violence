In 2024–25, 111 domestic homicides were recorded in England and Wales — a fall of 5.1% on the prior year, and a decline of 17.2% since 2014–15.

Three patterns in this file are worth sitting with. First, the female-victim–male-partner category isn't just the largest — it's the only one that has held that rank in every single year of the series, which suggests a structural risk rather than a fluctuating one. Second, the fall in overall numbers is real, but it's being driven almost entirely by that same category declining, not by any broad improvement across the board. Third, and most concerning to me: the justice-gap trend on page four has narrowed sharply in the latest year, but only because convictions fell, not because charging improved — that's a closing gap for the wrong reason, and worth flagging to anyone reading the headline number alone.

— Analyst, ONS-DHI desk reviewed

Counts by year, acquainted victims only. Down 18.6% since 2014–15, with the sharpest single-year drop in 2024–25.

Among victims who were acquainted with their suspect, 59.3% of homicides in 2024–25 were sparked by quarrel, revenge, or loss of temper — not premeditation or financial motive. This remains the dominant circumstance throughout the eleven-year series, though its share has eased from the 2018–19 peak.

Methodology

Chain of custody for the figures in this file — from source workbook to the tables and charts shown above.

Source

All figures are drawn from the ONS Homicide Index, appendix tables for the year ending March 2025. The original file is a single, heavily-merged Excel workbook with one worksheet per breakdown — sex pairings, victim relationships, suspect outcomes, and circumstances — each formatted for print rather than analysis.

Processing chain

  1. 01Extraction. Each worksheet was read with pandas, targeting the fixed row ranges that hold the actual count cells, since headers and notes vary sheet to sheet.
  2. 02Reshaping. Merged, wide-format cells (one row per category, one column per year) were unpivoted into long-format rows of category, year, count — the structure every chart and table on this site is built from.
  3. 03Footnote handling. ONS appends bracketed footnote markers to cells ([note 12], [x] for suppressed small counts). These were stripped with regular expressions; suppressed cells were set to zero rather than dropped, and are flagged in the underlying CSVs rather than silently hidden.
  4. 04Verification. Reshaped totals were checked against the published ONS summary commentary before anything was treated as final.
One issue surfaced during cleaning and is worth recording rather than quietly fixing: an early pass on the suspect-outcomes worksheet mis-set the starting column, which shifted every year's count one column to the left and understated the most recent year while overstating the earliest. It was caught because the resulting "2014–15 murder convictions" figure didn't match the equivalent number in the ONS's own published commentary — a reminder that anything in this file should ultimately trace back to a number ONS has stated themselves, not just to a script that ran without error.

What this file does not contain

Counts under official suppression thresholds, geographic detail below the England-and-Wales level, and any breakdown the published tables don't already provide (the circumstances and suspect-outcomes tables, for instance, are not split by victim sex at source — the filter control on this site is therefore inert on those two pages, by design rather than oversight).
